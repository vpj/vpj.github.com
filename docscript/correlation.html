Correlation Test

Correlation test observes if the payments to a vendor has a similar pattern compared to payments to other vendors.

Example Findings

Ghost Vendors: Fake vendors where there is no co-relation to the average vendor pattern.

Transportation cost should correlate with company’s production pattern. Labor cost should correlate with company’s production pattern.

Correlation

The correlation between the payment pattern to a particular vendor and total payments to all vendors is calculated. The correlation is a figure between -1 and +1.

  • Correlation of +1

    A correlation of +1 means that the pattern of payments to the vendor and the pattern of total payments match 100%.

    That is, when payments to the vendor are low total payments are low, and when payments to the vendor are high total payments are high. In other words, the graphs of the two payments patterns can be coincided with each other if one of them is scaled and shifted.

  • Correlation of -1

    This is the exact opposite of +1 correlation.

    The payments to the vendor exactly match the inverted total payment pattern. Payments to the vendor are high when total payments are low, and payments to the vendor are low when total payments are high.

  • Correlation of 0

    A correlation of 0 signals that vendor payment pattern has no relationship to total payment pattern.

    The plot below shows an example of a near-zero correlation.

Interpreting Results

  • High Positive Correlation (Greater than +0.3)

    These are probably the main suppliers such as raw material suppliers and suppliers of other related goods or services such as transportation of raw materials, temporary labor and import/export tax.

    The risk level is low and hence the need for investigation is low.

  • Low Positive Correlation (Less than +0.1)

    Usually, these are the vendors who have a fixed supply with some component of variability based on the production rates of the company. Examples are electricity, water and tools supply. Also, the supplies with high levels of stocks will fall into this category.

    These can also be considered as low risk, and only investigated if the type of vendor does not fall into this category.

  • Zero Correlation (Between -0.1 and +0.1)

    Zero correlation usually happens with vendors who supply at a fixed rate, for example office supplies. Fixed asset supplies such as new machinery also will have a near zero correlation.

    Anything other than these should be investigated, as there might be delayed and advance payments to manipulate accounts and fraudulent payments to fake vendors.

  • Negative Correlation (Less than -0.1)

    The level of risk is high. Maintenance might fall into this category since it is done when the machinery have free time. If there are products manufactured during seasons when the demand for the main products, and vendors related to those will fall into this category.

    Any vendor in this category is worth being investigated, to find fraud or at least to get insights into payment patterns.

Correlation Test on Forestpin

Click the VENDORS: PAYMENT VALUE tile and select a vendor (Woodville in this example), and then click the show data related only to that specific vendor.

Now you should see the main screen results for the picked vendor, Woodville, as shown below:

Pick on the PAYMENT CORRELATION ANALYSIS test and you will see results of the correlation test as shown below. This test shows the correlation between all vendor invoices and the vendor Woodville.

The blue bars at the bottom represent the total invoice value by month. The whitelines on the top shows if both are totally correlated (Correlation +1) the amount of purchasing that should have been done. The bars on top show the actuals.

If the bars are green, then the vendor invoices are in a similar pattern to the total; If the bars are in red, it's the opposite.
